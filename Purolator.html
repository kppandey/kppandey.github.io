Purolator

Route Optimization

I developed and led a project to develop an application to automate and simplify route planning.

About Purolator

Purolator is one of the oldest courier companies in Canada. It is a leading freight, package and logistics provider. As it's considered a local Canadian company, they have been fortunate to have long-term employees which has contributed to their success in the freight industry. When I joined the Operations team, they had just began strategizing on how to retain tribal knowledge. To learn more about the benefits of this project, read here!

Problem

There were two problems that we wanted to prioritize:

  1. The reliance of tribal knowledge across the warehouses.
  2. Manual delivery and pick-up routes planning.

Design Process

The main problem I was trying to solve was: How can we automate route planning and make it more efficient?

In order to develop an application for route planning, I needed to understand how route managers created routes in the first place. I conducted various usability testing and personas to better understand the "users" or in this case, the route managers for this product. I also shadowed and interviewed route managers to determine what decisions they use to create routes in order to understand their thought process.

Design Iterations

  1. Research other freight companies and their route planning solutions: I met with Canada Post and UPS to better understand how their companies have automated route planning
  2. Strategizing with the higher-ups, to determine the cheapest, less costly application that we can build to automate route planning.
  3. Create low-fidelity mockups to understand the outline of each interface that we wanted to develop.
  4. Designed high-fidelity mockups and collaborated both with the engineering team and well as the fields team including route managers to determine the best designs.

Challenges

Constant feedback is very important.
I learned that whether it's from the engineering team or the field, feedback is crucial when designing and developing a product. Additionally, I learned that you should always go back to the "Why" of the project. Whenever I had difficulty with a design decision, I learned to ask myself does this decision help in the problem we're trying to solve? Would this decision make route planning efficient and usable for route managers + less costly?
